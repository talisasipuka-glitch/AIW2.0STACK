# 07 — Money Math (Motorsport / Automotive: Performance & Tuning Shops)

Synthesis from Sub-task 3 (vehicle-specific packages, financing offers), Sub-task 6 (PAA cost data for ECU/dyno tuning), and Sub-task 1/5 (one-stop-shop and repeat-customer framing). Currency: USD.

---

## Average ticket size

Unlike the personal-injury niche (one large contingency fee per case) or real estate (one commission per transaction), a performance/tuning shop's revenue comes from a **range of individually-priced jobs**, from small to large:

- **Basic ECU tune**: $200 to $500 (per Sub-task 6's PAA data).
- **Advanced/custom ECU tuning, Stage 2+**: $500 to $1,500.
- **Larger jobs** — exhaust/downpipe installs, supercharger installs, suspension/coilover packages, custom fabrication, "Power Packages by Vehicle" (Sub-task 3/4) — realistically run **$1,000 to $5,000+** per job, with full custom builds going well beyond that.

A reasonable blended planning figure for a **typical job ticket across this customer base is roughly $800 to $1,500** — weighted toward tuning and mid-tier upgrade jobs rather than full custom builds, which are less frequent but high-value outliers (analogous to the personal-injury niche's catastrophic cases).

## Repeat-customer dynamics (the niche's unique multiplier)

This niche has a structural advantage the other two researched niches don't: **enthusiast customers build their cars in stages**. A customer who gets a Stage 1 ECU tune often returns later for an exhaust, then suspension, then a Stage 2 tune after further mods (Sub-task 3's "Cars we tune" and "Performance Packages by Vehicle" structures both imply a staged-upgrade path). A single end customer is realistically worth **2 to 4x their first ticket** over the life of a build, before counting routine maintenance/dyno re-tunes after each new modification.

## Estimated close rate per qualified lead

Because the niche's primary on-page CTA is a **vehicle selector** (Sub-task 4) — the visitor self-identifies their exact car before any contact exchange — a lead reaching the contact form has already pre-qualified themselves (their car is supported, and they've expressed interest in a specific package or service). This is a less ambiguous qualification step than the personal-injury niche's "is this a viable case" screening. A working planning assumption is **roughly 1 in 3 (30-35%) of qualified, vehicle-identified leads convert to a paying job** — higher than the personal-injury niche's 20-25% sign rate, because there's no legal-viability gate, only fit/budget/scheduling.

## Leads needed per month for a meaningful outcome

To generate a meaningful incremental outcome for a small performance shop — say, **5 to 7 new jobs per month** at the $800-$1,500 blended ticket (roughly $4,000 to $10,000 in monthly revenue from new jobs alone) — at a 30-35% close rate, the shop needs roughly **15 to 20 qualified, vehicle-identified leads per month** from the website.

## Money math headline

"If your site gets you 15-20 people a month who tell us their car and what they want done, and you close about 1 in 3 of them, that's 5-7 new jobs a month — at a typical ticket of $800 to $1,500, that's $4,000 to $10,000 a month in new work. And because builds happen in stages, a good chunk of those customers come back for the next stage, so the website keeps paying off long after the first job."
