# 05 — Trust Signals (Personal Injury Lawyers)

Synthesis from Sub-tasks 1 (agencies) and 4 (CRO patterns), cross-referenced against the end-customer voice in Sub-task 2 and the universal Trust SOP (B1) in `website-factory/references/cro-sops/universal-cro-sops.md`.

## Top 5 trust signals, in priority order

### 1. "No Win, No Fee" / contingency guarantee, stated explicitly and repeatedly
**Justification:** This is the single most repeated line across every crawled homepage (Sub-task 3 and 4) — "No Win, No Fee. Guaranteed.", "The Fee Is Free™. Only pay if we win.", "You owe us nothing until we recover compensation." Sub-task 2 identified "cost of hiring a lawyer before knowing if it's worth it" as a top-5 fear; this single statement directly removes that barrier and should appear near every primary CTA, not just once on the page.

### 2. Settlement/dollar-amount proof, tagged by case type
**Justification:** Dolman's $6.7M/$5M/$3.2M/$3.85M grid and Omar Ochoa's "$1 Billion Recovered" headline (Sub-task 3 and 4) let the end customer match their own injury type to a real number, making the firm's competence concrete rather than a vague claim like "best lawyers." Per Sub-task 1, this is the dominant proof currency at the agency-to-firm level too — it has clearly become the niche's baseline expectation.

### 3. Communication / responsiveness proof, ideally with a named-person guarantee
**Justification:** Sub-task 2's review analysis showed "kept me updated" and "answered any questions" as the most-repeated positive themes (5 of 8 reviews each), with the implied fear being "being ignored or left in the dark" — the #1 fear identified. Dolman's testimonials calling out "personal cell phone access to the partner" (Sub-task 3) is the strongest concrete answer to this fear seen in the research, and should be a template pattern: a stated communication commitment (e.g. "you'll always know who's handling your case and how to reach them"), not just a generic "we care" claim.

### 4. "Not a settlement mill" / fight-for-you positioning
**Justification:** Both Sub-task 1 (agencies positioning PI firms against "settlement mills") and Sub-task 3/4 (Omar Ochoa's "We are not a settlement mill," Dolman's "Many TV firms are settlement mills — we're litigation bulldogs") show this as a recurring differentiator. Sub-task 2's fear #2 ("not getting enough compensation / being lowballed") is the end-customer-facing version of this same concern — the end customer doesn't use the phrase "settlement mill," but they fear exactly what it describes: a firm that pushes a fast, cheap settlement rather than fighting for full value.

### 5. Free, low-friction first consultation, framed as informational (not a sales call)
**Justification:** "Free Case Evaluation" / "Free Case Review" appears on every crawled site (Sub-task 3 and 4) as the dominant CTA. Sub-task 2 shows the firm's own marketing ("we offer free initial consultations to help you understand your rights and your options") directly targets fear #3 (not understanding the legal process) and fear #4 (cost before knowing value). Framing this as "understand your options" rather than "talk to sales" lowers the activation energy for an end customer who is injured, stressed, and time-pressed.

## Niche-specific certifications / badges

- **State bar admission / license number** (Sub-task 3: Dolman links directly to Florida Bar profile and federal court admission pages) — baseline credibility, especially important since the end customer is choosing among thousands of similarly-worded firms (12,572 PI attorneys in NY, 6,334 in TX per Sub-task 1).
- **"Million Dollar Advocates Forum" / "Top 100 High Stakes Litigators" / "National Trial Lawyers"** (Sub-task 3, Dolman) — niche-specific award/membership badges that function as a "we've actually won big cases" proof layer beyond the firm's own claimed numbers.
- **"As seen in" media logos** (Inc., Forbes, Entrepreneur, People, HuffPost, Bloomberg for Dolman; ABC/CNN/Fox/NBC for Morgan & Morgan) — Sub-task 4 shows these appear immediately after the hero, before any body copy, making press credibility one of the very first things the end customer sees.
- **Google review aggregate (star rating + count)** — Sub-task 2's primary data source (PM Law Firm, 4.9 stars / 627 reviews) shows this volume-plus-rating combination is itself a trust signal, distinct from the individual review content.
- **Spanish-speaking / bilingual service badge** (Sub-task 3, Omar Ochoa) — directly relevant to the NY/Houston/LA target markets, signals accessibility to a large share of the end-customer population in those cities.
- **Bar association / community involvement badges** ("In the Community" section, Sub-task 4 screenshot of omarochoalaw.com) — localized trust signal showing the firm is embedded in the community it serves, not a remote ad-funded operation.

## Press / "as seen in" expectations

- National media logos (Inc., Forbes, People, ABC, CNN, Fox, NBC) are table-stakes for the larger/established firms in this niche (Sub-task 3 and 4) — for smaller firms without this access, local news features or local "best of" lists (e.g. "Best PI Lawyers in Houston") serve the same structural role.
- Case-result press coverage (a major settlement covered by local news) functions as a hybrid of trust signal #2 (dollar proof) and press credibility — worth flagging to clients as a marketing asset if they have one.

## Photo / video testimonial expectations

- **Named attorney with face on the page**: every successful homepage in Sub-task 3 and 4 features a named, photographed attorney (Matthew Dolman, Omar Ochoa, "John Morgan") — not an anonymous "our team" graphic. This directly supports trust signal #3 (a real person the end customer can reach).
- **Founder/attorney personal story**: Dolman's origin story (his father's accident, a bad lawyer experience, "I've been in your shoes") is the single strongest emotional trust element found across both niches researched so far — converts a generic "why choose us" into a specific, relatable narrative that mirrors the end customer's own likely situation.
- **Client testimonials with photos, framed around the case-type and outcome** (e.g. Omar Ochoa's video testimonial "Maribel Vega") — Sub-task 2 shows story-based, specific testimonials outperform generic star ratings; the niche template should pair a dollar amount or case type with each testimonial where possible (e.g. "Car accident victim, recovered $X — 'they kept me updated the whole time'").
- **Case results presented as a grid/table with dollar amounts and brief case descriptions** (Dolman's "Results We Get" section) — this is the niche's equivalent of "before/after" photos in visual trades; it is the primary evidence format end customers are shown.
